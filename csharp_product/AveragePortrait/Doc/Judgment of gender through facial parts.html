Abstract. Japanese male and female undergraduate students judged the gender of a variety of
facial images. These images were combinations of the following facial parts: eyebrows, eyes,
nose, mouth, and the face outline (cheek and chin). These parts were extracted from averaged
facial images of Japanese males and females aged 18 and 19 years by means of the Facial Image
Processing System. The results suggested that, in identifying gender, subjects performed iden-
tification on the basis of the eyebrows and the face outline, and both males and females were
more likely to identify the faces as those of their own gender. The results are discussed in rela-
tion to previous studies, with particular attention paid to the matter of race differences.
1 Introduction
Identifying gender is important for social organisms such as humans, not only for the
purpose of reproduction but also for maintaining their social life. Many species of
primates, including humans, have developed sexual dimorphism capable of helping
this fundamental need of social identification depending upon visual-information
processing.
With humans, the face plays the most important role in transmitting visual informa-
tion from one person to another. Facial information enables us to estimate another
person's emotional state and to identify a person's race, age, health, and so on. With
respect to gender, however, this does not necessarily mean that humans identify
gender only on the basis of the face; gender cues include a variety of characteristics,
such as body size, thickness of the chest, width of the shoulder, gait pattern
(Kozlowski and Cutting 1977); additionally clothes and cosmetics are factors.
Our actual identification of gender is undoubtedly based on the combination of all
of these. Our first question here is whether Japanese undergraduate students are able
to identify gender solely on the basis of facial features. If they are, then the second
question is how they do so. There are many possible cues employed to identify
gender: eyes, eyebrows, nose, mouth, and the face outline. We want to answer the
question "Which of these cues play important roles in the identification of gender?".
Roberts and Bruce (1988) assessed the effects of masking the eyes, the nose, and
the mouth on the speed and accuracy of gender judgment. The results were that
concealing the nose increased the reaction time of gender identification, though
subjects could not judge the gender by the nose alone. Roberts and Bruce suggested
that the relationship between the size and shape of the nose provides useful facial
information in judging gender. In their next study (Bruce et al 1993), masks conceal-
ing the eye region reduced the accuracy of gender judgments the most. The greater
effect of masking the eyes compared with masking the nose appears inconsistent with
the effect reported by them earlier (Roberts and Bruce 1988), in which masking the
eyes had a much smaller effect on gender judgments than masking the nose.

In the study by Roberts and Bruce (1988), masks that concealed the nose also
concealed the eyebrows. However, in the Bruce et al (1993) study, masks concealing
the nose did not conceal any portion of the eyebrow region, whereas masks conceal-
ing the eyes also concealed the eyebrows. In one experiment, visibility of the central
brow region had little effect on judgments of gender (Bruce et al 1993). In the other
experiment, judging gender in three-dimensional (3-D) representations of faces with-
out skin texture was less accurate than in photographs of faces, especially for female
faces (Bruce et al 1993). This may suggest that features such as eyebrows and skin
texture were more important cues to gender discrimination than the 3-D representa-
tion itself. From results of face measurements (Burton et al 1993), the gap separating
the eyebrows was one of the cues used to discriminate gender. These studies indi-
cated that the facial cues for gender discrimination are the eyebrows and the nose.
We found the following problems in the previous studies: (1) the masked faces were
unnatural, that is, in our daily life we do not see such strange faces; and (2) the varia-
tion of faces among individuals was ignored. There are many kinds of variations in
faces: feminine or masculine faces, distinctive faces or not, typical faces or not, etc.
In order to study the measurement of faces, we must deal with such variations of
faces. However, in order to clarify the most effective factor of the face experimentally,
we think we must make an effort to minimize these variations of faces in experimental
studies. These problems may be resolved through computer-assisted stimulus-
generating systems. With advanced computer systems, we are able to 'average' facial
images and to modify facial parts by using image-processing systems, without any loss
of naturalness in the faces. By using these averaged facial images we can minimize
individual facial differences. In this paper we examine the validity of averaged
Japanese faces by comparing the results of averaged Caucasian faces (Brown and
Perrett 1993) and averaged Japanese faces.
Recently, some psychologists have suggested the possibility of using "computer-
generated composite facial prototypes" in psychological research (Benson and Perrett
1991, 1992). These methods enable psychologists to study the judgment of gender
through faces. At the present stage, these averaged-face studies are being carried out
only with Caucasian faces, though it is important to use these techniques to study the
differences between as well as the universality of averaged faces of different races.
Recently, only Perrett et al (1994) have reported on a comparison of judgment of
female facial attractiveness between two races. These authors referred to female
attractiveness across different cultural backgrounds; however, they did not mention
the differences between male and female faces across different cultural backgrounds.
In this paper, to study the differences between averaged Caucasian faces (Brown and
Perrett 1993) and averaged Mongoloid faces, we first made averaged faces of young
Japanese students and, second, had Japanese students judge these faces.
The points which we want to confirm aie as follows.
(i)Can Japanese subjects judge gender through averaged Japanese faces? In other
words, can averaged faces represent gender? The main purpose is to confirm the
validity of using averaged Japanese faces in gender judgments.
(ii) Which facial parts are most important in judging gender in Japanese averaged
faces? Can results from a study of Japanese agree with those of a study of Causasians?
(iii) Are there differences in ability between subjects judging gender? As in Brown
and Perrett's study (1993), do female subjects tend to judge male faces as male, and
do male subjects tend to judge female faces as female?
By means of the Facial Image Processing System (Yamada et al 1992), the present
experiment was carried out as follows. First, we generated averaged male and female
facial images. Second, we created artificial facial images by combining facial parts

2 Method
2.1 Subjects
Subjects who rated the images were seventy-two male and fifty female undergraduate
students. Thirty-two male and forty female students were from the Department of
Psychology of Chuo University, and forty male and ten female students were from the
Department of Engineering of Gunma University. They were either 18 or 19 years old.
All subjects were Japanese.
2.2 Stimulus
Color photographs of faces were taken of twenty-six male and twenty-six female
Japanese undergraduate students (18-19 years old) from the Department of Psychology
of Chuo University. They were not the same students as those who were subjects
judging faces. All photographs displayed a whole face with neutral expression. No
makeup was worn.
These photographs were mixed to construct averaged facial images by using the
Facial Image Processing System (Yamada et al 1992). The averaging procedure
consisted of two independent procedures: (1) averaging the form of the wire-frame
model (ie averaging the values of the 3-D coordinates of each point making up the
wire-frame model) and (2) averaging the brightness of three primary colors of pixels
making up the two-dimensional (2-D) patterns of the face surfaces. The 3-D wire-
frame model was composed of many 2-D triangular areas. In the first step the form
of each triangle was changed and in the second step the color of the inside of each
triangle was changed. The standard wire-frame model was transformed to fit the
target face by manually changing the fitting points. The wire-frame model was able to
make up for deficiencies in the data in a 2-D photograph to be transferred to 3-D
data. Using the wire-frame model, we could automatically normalize the size, pose,
and angle of each face. Conventionally, to make average faces, all faces had to have
the same size, pose, and angle. But the wire-frame model does not require all faces to
be of exactly the same size or be at the same angle.
Last, we made five types of facial images. We averaged twenty-six male faces and
created a male prototype. We also averaged twenty-six female faces and created a
female prototype. Furthermore, twenty-six male and twenty-six female faces were
averaged together to produce an androgyne. In the same manner as Brown and
Perrett (1993), we doubled the differences between the feature points of the male
prototype and the female prototype and created a 'hyper male' and a 'hyper female'.
After the averaging procedure, we swapped corresponding facial parts between the
female prototype and the male prototype. The same procedure was followed between
the hyper male and the hyper female. In the Facial Image Processing System, the
replaced parts had already been defined according to the wire-frame model. If we
could simply select the parts, we could swap the facial parts automatically. These parts
were the eyebrows, eyes, nose, mouth, and the face outline (ie cheek and chin). Figure 1
shows the parts that were chosen. If a replaced facial part and parts of the original
face showed quite a difference in color, red, green, and blue pixels around the edges
of the replaced regions were automatically blended to eliminate the boundary lines.
Hair was excluded from these stimuli. This is because the purpose in this paper is
only to determine whether we can judge gender from facial cues. The forehead was
cut around. Because of variations in the width of the forehead, the top of the
forehead showed unnatural shadows of hair. To conceal the shadows and fix the
stimulus conditions, we manually trimmed away the top of the forehead of each

stimulus so that they were all of the same size. The ears were also excluded in this
study because some ears were hidden by hair. In this system, all facial images were
standardized in terms of size. That is, we equalized the triangular areas whose apexes
were the two inner corners of the eyes and the top of the nose. This process was
done automatically.
In our system, analog data (a photograph of each face) was transformed to digital
data by a component transcoder (Homei/CT600) and an image-processing device
(Nexus/Nexus6800). The main processing was done by a workstation (Sun/
SPARCstationl). The digital data (the facial images that were produced) were then
transformed to analog data by the same machines. The analog data were shown on a
TV monitor and printed by a video printer (SONY/UP-5150). These pictures were
made into slides. Some of the finished stimuli are shown in figure 2. None of the
stimuli lost quality to a serious degree.

2.3 Procedure
The subjects were divided into three groups to counterbalance the effect of the order of
the stimuli. The experiments were done three times according to the same procedure.
All subjects were seated in an audiovisual-education room and the general proce-
dure was explained to them. The facial images, including those shown in figure 2,
were projected onto an 80 cm x 60 cm screen through a slide projector. The stimuli
were shown one by one. The duration of each stimulus image was 10 s. Interstimulus
intervals were 20 s. The subjects were asked to judge the gender of the projected
images and to write down their answers (male or female) on sheets of paper during
this 30 s period. All facial images were shown only once. Subjects were tested in a
group. All groups consisted of about fifteen subjects. At the viewing distance of
about 200 cm, each face subtended a retinal angle of about 8 deg high by 7 deg wide.
The stimuli consisted of androgynous, male prototype, female prototype, hyper
male, hyper female, and other faces. These other faces were made by substituting
parts of one prototype (or hyper) for corresponding parts of the other prototype (or
hyper); these parts included eyebrows, eyes, nose, mouth, outline, and combinations
of two parts selected from the five parts. There was a total number of sixty-five
stimuli. The stimuli were presented in a random order.

3 Results
3.1 Results for prototype faces
3.1.1 Can Japanese undergraduates judge gender only from the face? Figure 3 shows the
percentage of perceived male for androgynous, male prototype, female prototype,
hyper male, and hyper female faces. 95.8% of the male subjects and 94.0% of the
female subjects identified the male prototype as male. 11.3% of the male subjects and
4.0% of the female subjects misidentified the female prototype as male. Because all
answers were chosen between male and female, 88.7% of the male subjects and
96.0% of the female subjects identified the female prototype as female. These results,
for gender judgments of the male prototype and female prototype, were significantly
higher than the chance level (male prototype: %\ = 98.19, p < 0.001; female proto-
type: xl = 85.28, p < 0.001). The judgment of gender for the androgynous face was
close to the chance level; the judgments as male were approximately half of those for
the male prototype and as female half those for the female prototype.
The results for the hyper male and hyper female were almost the same as the
results for the male prototype and female prototype. 100% of the male and female
subjects identified the hyper male as male. 1.4% of the male subjects and 0% of the
female subjects misidentified the hyper female as male. Because all answers were
chosen between male and female, 98.6% of the male subjects and 100% of the female
subjects identified the female prototype as female. In our study, the prototype male
and prototype female exemplified their own gender well enough to produce a ceiling
effect. Therefore, the effects of extrapolating male and female features could not be
found in judging gender in our experiment. These results suggest that Japanese
undergraduates can identify gender by averaged faces without other gender cues.

3.2.4 Two-part substitutions between hyper faces. In the hyper male and hyper female
faces, the substitutions of eyebrows and eyes, eyebrows and nose, eyebrows and
mouth, eyebrows and outline, eyes and outline, and nose and outline had strong effects
on changing gender judgment. That is, the proportions of perceived male for these
faces were significantly lower than for the hyper male, and the proportions of
perceived female for these faces were significantly lower than for the hyper female.
Only in the hyper female did mouth and outline have the same effects. These results
suggest that the eyebrows and the face outline had an important effect on gender
judgment.
The proportion of subjects who correctly identified the hyper female with male
eyebrows was significantly lower than the proportion of subjects who correctly iden-
tified the hyper female with male outline (%\ = 24.94, p < 0.001). But the results of
the hyper male with female eyebrows and hyper male with female outline did not
show very different proportions {%\ = 7.88, p < 0.1). These results for the hyper
faces are opposite to the results for the prototype faces. In the prototype faces,
swapping eyebrows had a stronger effect than swapping the face outline only in the
male prototype. But in the hyper faces, swapping eyebrows had a stronger effect than
swapping the face outline only in the hyper female. These results have two inter-
pretations. One is that the eyebrows of the female prototype and the eyebrows of the
hyper male represent their own gender. The second is that, from figure 6, it seems
that the hyper male has a very strong effect and reaches the ceiling, so that differ-
ences in the effect of substituting parts are not shown in the hyper male. This matter
requires quantitative study.

3.4 Which facial images are identified more often as male or female?
In Brown and Perret's (1993) study, results for the male prototype were less correct
than those for the female prototype. In our experiment, however, there were no
differences in the rate of correct answers for gender judgment between the prototype
male and prototype female faces. 69.8% of the total judgments were female for all
prototype female faces (including faces that were the female prototype with male
parts), and 67.9% of the total judgments were male for all male prototype faces
(including faces that were the male prototype with female parts); there was no signifi-
cant difference between the judgments of prototype male and prototype female faces
{xl = 1.72). On the contrary, there were differences in-the judgments of gender
between hyper male and hyper female faces. 62.6% of the total judgments were
female for all hyper female faces (including faces that were the hyper female with
male parts), and 84.7% of the total judgments were male for all hyper male faces
(including faces that were the hyper male with female parts); there were significant
differences between the judgments of hyper male and hyper female faces (xl = 245.13,
p < 0.001).
In comparison of the prototype male and hyper male, the total percentage of
perceived male for all hyper male faces was higher than for all prototype male faces
(xl = 153.12, p < 0.001). But in comparison of the prototype female and hyper
female, the total percentage of perceived female for all hyper female faces was lower
than that for all prototype female faces (xl = 22A3, p < 0.001). The hyper female
and hyper male were made by using the same physical distance from each prototype
face, although psychological judgments were different. It is considered that these
gender judgments were not simple reflections of physical distances. To clarify this
point another experiment is needed.
With the faces with substituted parts, we also found such differences. The proportion
of subjects who judge the male prototype with female outline as male was significantly
higher than the proportion of subjects who judged the female prototype with male
outline as female (xl = 4.51, p < 0.05). Thus, replacing the outline with that of the
other gender had a smaller effect for the prototype male than for the prototype
female. In the hyper faces, the brows and outline also showed the same difference in
effects between the hyper male and hyper female (brows: xl = 54.31; outline:
xl = 26.99; p < 0.001 in each case). Replacing the outline and eyebrows had a smaller
effect for the hyper male than for the hyper female.
With regard to the substitution of eyebrows, the proportion of subjects who judged
the hyper male with female eyebrows as the original gender was higher than for the
prototype male with female eyebrows (xl = 52.55, p < 0.001), and that for the hyper
female with male eyebrows was lower than that for the prototype female with male
eyebrows (xl = 12.98, p < 0.001). From these results, we may say that in the eye-
brow region, a doubling of the difference between the feature points of the male and
female does not have the same effects in the psychological judgments of gender.

4 Discussion
The results suggest that (1) Japanese undergraduates can identity the gender of
Japanese people solely on the basis of face with averaged features; (2) the eyebrows
and the face outline are important cues in identifying the gender by facial images;
(3) the subjects tend to judge the facial images as their own gender; and (4) there is
no difference between the proportion of subjects who judged all prototype female
faces as the original gender and that for all prototype male faces. But in hyper faces,
the proportion of subjects who judged all hyper male faces as the original gender was
higher than that for all hyper female faces. Also, in female faces, the proportion of
subjects who judged all hyper female faces as the original gender was lower than that
for all prototype female faces. Therefore, parts of hyper faces do not heighten the
judgment of gender in female faces, especially in the eyebrow region. As a result,
hyper features do not have the same effect for male and female.
There were two main differences between the previous study on Caucasian proto-
type faces (Brown and Perrett 1993) and our study. The first was that the jaw was the
most effective part in judging gender in the previous study, but in our study eyebrows
had an equal effect to the face outline in the prototype female. Even in the prototype
male, the substitution of eyebrows had a stronger effect than the substitution of face
outline. In the strictest sense, the regions of facial parts were different between these
two studies. In the previous study the jaw and chin were divided, but in our study the
face outline area included the jaw, the chin, and even the cheek. Though the area of
the face outline was wider than the jaw, the effect of the face outline was weaker than
that of the jaw. This means that the eyebrows had a different effect in the two studies;
in our Japanese study the eyebrows had more of an effect than in the Caucasian
study. Second, in the previous study, the prototype male did not provide many
correct answers in judging gender, but our study did not show such an effect. We may
say that our Japanese prototype male was more masculine than the Caucasian proto-
type male. We think this difference is due to the color of the eyebrows. Because
Japanese eyebrows are black and in striking contrast to skin color, the thicker eyebrows
stand out more easily.
The eyebrows are also extremely important in signaling facial expressions. According
to Morris (1985), the eyebrows may have evolved mainly as signals to display gender
and age in our species. In addition, not only humans have such types of facial hair, but
many primates also have sex-specific facial hair (Guthrie 1970). For example, many
species of gibbon have white brow hair showing sexual dimorphism. The shape and
color of this hair is clearly different between male and female (Chivers 1984). So the
fact that eyebrows are important cues for judging gender can be accepted. We also
suspect that differences in the accuracy of gender judgment are due to the color of the
eyebrows. In Mongolian faces, the eyebrows are black and distinctive, so differences of
eyebrows are also distinctive, but this effect must be confirmed by more detailed studies.
In considering these race differences, the following two points must be taken into
account: (1) the method of using signals when judging gender in a face is different
between Japanese subjects and Causasian subjects, and (2) gender differences of facial
features are different between Japanese and Causasians. Shepherd (1981) suggested
that the identification of face by a subject of the same race was easier than that by a
subject of a different race. He also suggested that the strategy of face identification
(distinctive characteristics of faces) might be different among the different races. But
there has been no study clarifying racial differences in judging gender by face. Brown
and Perrett (1993) said that perceiving gender is subjective rather than absolute. If
this is true, then gender differences and race differences will affect the subjects' judg-
ment of faces. This is because the method of judging gender will be different. We
have to investigate other races further to clarify these points.
